# Telling data-driven stories about documents and text

The same seven angles can be used to tell stories about text-based data as about any other form of data. We can tell stories about: 

* Scale: how often words/phrases are used
* Change: how language has changed
* Ranking: the most/least common words/phrases
* Variation: e.g. by gender, ethnicity etc.
* Exploratory: journeys through multiple angles
* Relationships: e.g. right vs left wing
* Leads: the ‘smoking gun’

Here are some examples:

## Stories about change

*The war in Ukraine has made Russian social-media users glum* is just one example of using data journalism techniques to reveal a change in language: "Posts on Twitter are far sadder today than at the start of the covid-19 pandemic," The Economist [reported in its 2022 article](https://www.economist.com/graphic-detail/2022/03/12/the-war-in-ukraine-has-made-russian-social-media-users-glum). 

Strictly speaking, the headline *'Hope’ is out, ‘fight’ is in: Does tweeting divide Congress, or simply echo its division?”* is an **exploratory** one, because it raises a question that the article promises to answer. But the question focuses on change, and the standfirst underneath the headline leads on the change that it found: "USA TODAY analysed more than 2.8 million tweets posted by members of Congress. The language has become more divided and emotional".

The story [won an award](https://nationalpress.org/award-story/usa-today-data-and-visual-journalists-win-innovative-storytelling-award/) but is no longer available on the USA Today website, or on the Wayback Machine. The only version I could find is a [Yahoo copy](https://www.yahoo.com/news/hope-fight-does-tweeting-divide-100040058.html) which is missing visuals or that standfirst. Instead look at the award page and [watch the video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Vexb0dx7zQ0) to see the article. 

## Ranking stories

Again, *Sing My Name* is not explicitly a ranking angle to [this Pudding feature](https://pudding.cool/2019/05/names-in-songs/), and the standfirst is more exploratory: "If your name is Baby, then seemingly every song is about you. But what if your name isn’t Baby?"

But the sections in this feature focus a lot on ranking: what are the most popular names in songs; which songs have the most repeat mentions of the same name; what songs contain the most names; and so on. 

## Exploratory stories

Most data journalism using text as a source is exploratory, for a few reasons: firstly, analysing text is typically more resource-intensive than working with data that is already structured, which is unlikely to be justified if the resulting story is a simple news article. Secondly, text is a rich resource that can yield multiple insights, which suits a more exploratory feature format. And finally, text is often complex, lending itself more to exploration, while the analysis itself will often require explanation too. 

Here are some examples:

* [*What 1.2 million parliamentary speeches can teach us about gender representation.*](https://pudding.cool/2018/07/women-in-parliament/) from US data journalism specialists The Pudding takes a common exploratory approach of looking at the text data along various thematic lines (each of which forms a chapter in the story)
* [*#SONA2022: How does it compare to the last 5 speeches by Ramaphosa?*](https://web.archive.org/web/20220211154430/https://theoutlier.co.za/news/82289/sona2022-how-does-it-compare-to-the-last-5-speeches-by-ramaphosa) by South African data journalism website The Outlier does the same
* [*How much violence is in Rammstein's lyrics?*](https://www.spiegel.de/kultur/musik/till-lindemann-wie-viel-gewalt-steckt-in-rammsteins-texten-a-b6e0820c-2b2f-4366-b6aa-84cc162f5665?giftToken=3a86d996-6673-405c-9a6e-899f1aa05f68) by German newspaper Der Spiegel uses another common device: ask a question
